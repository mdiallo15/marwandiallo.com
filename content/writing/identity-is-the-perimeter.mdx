I spent my first five years in tech moving mailboxes.

That's not a metaphor. I was an Active Directory and Exchange guy. I did the unglamorous migrations — the 2003 to 2010, the 2010 to Online, the hybrid configs nobody wanted to touch. I knew what a KCC was. I could read a `repadmin /showrepl` and tell you which site link was about to die.

The people doing "security" were somewhere upstairs.

When I eventually made the move into security, the thing that struck me was how little of what I'd learned downstairs was wasted. In fact, most of it became the job.

---

## The story we tell ourselves

The conventional story goes: firewalls were the perimeter, then cloud killed the perimeter, then zero trust replaced the perimeter with identity. You've heard it five hundred times. I've said it five hundred times.

It's not wrong. It's just not specific enough to do anything with.

Here's what most people miss. "Identity" is not a concept. It's a row in a table. Usually it's a refresh token sitting in a browser profile on someone's personal laptop. The person owns the laptop. The laptop is at a Starbucks. The token is valid for 14 more days. That is the perimeter. That. Right there.

When a real incident starts, you don't find yourself in a Palo Alto dashboard. You find yourself in Entra sign-in logs, trying to decide whether a successful MFA satisfaction at 3:17 a.m. from a Lagos IP is the CFO on vacation or an AITM proxy that already replayed the cookie.

The perimeter is a person making a decision about whether to tap a prompt on a phone they're holding in a kitchen.

## What changes when you accept this

Three things.

**First**, your attack tree collapses. The phishing email, the OAuth consent grant, the token theft from a stealer, the help-desk social-engineering call — they're all the same attack. They're all "I am going to become someone you trust." Everything else is delivery.

**Second**, your controls list shrinks. I used to write long defense-in-depth decks. Twelve layers. Seventeen layers. Now I write three:

1. Make the identity hard to steal. (Phishing-resistant MFA. Passkeys. Certificate-bound tokens.)
2. Make a stolen identity hard to use. (Conditional access. Device compliance. Continuous access evaluation.)
3. Make a compromised identity obvious fast. (Audit log. Lockout. Real-time alerts on a very short list of events.)

That's it. That's the program. Everything else is implementation detail.

**Third**, you stop caring about a lot of things you used to care about. Password complexity policies. Periodic rotation. Most of the old NIST 800-63 we were all trained on got walked back, and if you still have a 90-day rotation policy in 2026 you are making your users worse at security, not better. Long passphrases, a password manager, and phishing-resistant MFA — that's the floor. Everything above the floor is identity.

## Why passkeys actually matter

I got a little religious about passkeys when I was wiring them up for my own firm. Not because they're new and shiny, but because they collapse the attack tree I just described.

A passkey is a private key that never leaves the authenticator. It's cryptographically bound to the origin. You can't phish it. You can't replay it. You can't buy it from a stealer-log marketplace. A user typing the wrong domain into a lookalike proxy cannot present the passkey to the proxy, because the browser will not serve it to the wrong origin. The attack class just goes away.

That is rare. Most security tooling reduces risk at the margin. Passkeys delete a category.

The reason adoption is still slow is cultural, not technical. The recovery story is awkward, the cross-device UX is still rough, and every enterprise PM wants the option to fall back to SMS because some VP forgot their phone in a taxi. I get it. But the direction is settled.

## The thing under all of this

The thing I keep coming back to, after ten years, is that security is not really about systems. It's about the very small number of moments each day where a human makes a decision under uncertainty.

Did I actually send this request? Did I actually authorize this payment? Did I actually consent to this app?

Our job is to make the right answer cheap and the wrong answer visible. That's it. We dress it up with frameworks and acronyms — and I love a good framework — but the frameworks exist to make that one thing happen at scale.

The longer I do this, the more convinced I am that the firewall was a lie we told ourselves because it was tractable. A box you could buy and rack. Identity is the real problem, and identity is messy, because people are messy.

Good. That's where the interesting work is.
