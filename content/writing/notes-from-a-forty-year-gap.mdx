The last time I was in Conakry, the power went out three times during dinner.

This is not a complaint. The restaurant had a generator, everyone knew the rhythm, the conversation barely paused. My cousin laughed at my face the first time the lights cut and pointed out that I'd lived in America too long. He was right.

But I sat there thinking about the generator. Someone had to buy it, someone had to fuel it, someone had to maintain it — and there is one of these behind every functioning business in Conakry. The restaurant's margin, quietly, pays for a parallel electrical grid that shouldn't exist. Multiply that by every cafe, every clinic, every data center that won't get built because the compounding cost of running a generator for forty years is what tells an operator not to come.

That's what I want to write about. Not the poverty story — the infrastructure story.

## The gap

Guinea has roughly the same population as Rwanda, more natural wealth than most countries on the continent, and a coastline that any logistics engineer would love. The gap between what it is and what it could be is not a mystery and it is not, despite what the politics section of the paper tells you, mostly political. It's infrastructural.

What I mean by that: the foundational layers — reliable electricity, navigable roads, addressable land, consistent water, affordable bandwidth, identity documents that reconcile across systems — are the things other countries built between about 1945 and 1985. We are forty years behind on the foundation, which means every layer on top is harder to build than it should be.

A fintech in Conakry is not just a fintech. It's a fintech that has to run its own identity verification because national ID coverage is spotty, its own power redundancy because the grid isn't, its own settlement rails because the interbank system is slow, and its own customer education because most of its users have never used a smartphone app before this one. Each of those is the work. The product, the thing the engineers were hired to build, is the small visible part.

I've watched this pattern over and over. It's not that the people are less talented. It's that every step costs more.

## The diaspora problem

The diaspora wants to help. I want to help. Every Guinean I know abroad with any skill at all has, at some point, a version of "I should do something back home."

The diaspora is also usually bad at it. I say this as a member of the diaspora.

The reasons are structural. The person with the skill is not in the country. The person in the country with context is not the person with the skill. The person with capital is different from both. Coordinating the three, remotely, across time zones and incentives, with no on-the-ground accountability, is how you end up with a half-built school sitting unused for six years because somebody's cousin pocketed the cement money.

I've been trying to figure out what a good version of the diaspora contribution actually looks like. My current thinking:

**Small, boring, repeatable.** The cathedral projects fail. The things that work tend to be narrow — send a cohort through a real AWS course, underwrite a year of hosting for a local startup, pay for a kid's tuition. Things with a clear boundary and a clear accountable person.

**Leverage over transfer.** Five hundred dollars delivered as cash is five hundred dollars. Five hundred dollars delivered as three months of a cloud account and a mentor who takes a weekly call is worth multiples. The diaspora's asymmetric advantage is not the dollars — it's the judgement and the networks that can't be bought locally yet.

**Build the bridge, not the thing.** I do not need to go back and run a cybersecurity firm in Conakry. Someone there will do that better than I can. What I can do is be a bridge — introduce the founders there to the networks here, underwrite the first few years of tooling, help hire the first two people. The bridges compound. The buildings often don't.

## Why this is on my mind now

I'm at a point in my career where I could plausibly make a bet on this. Not next year — I'm realistic about the firm, about my day job, about the fact that I have a daughter and a mortgage and am not twenty-five anymore. But I can see the shape.

Somewhere in the next five years I think there's a version of Diallo Group that has a Conakry office. Maybe it's security, maybe it's broader. The people who run it are Guinean, not me. My role is capital, network, and the quiet insistence that the work be done to an institutional standard — that the reports be well-written, the portals be actually secure, the invoices go out on time. The boring things, which in a place still forty years behind the infrastructure curve are rarer and more valuable than the exciting things.

That's the shape I see.

## The thing I'm not willing to say

The thing I'm not willing to say online, and then I'm going to stop, is what I actually feel when I think about Guinea. It's not a clean feeling. It's a tangle.

Some of it is pride. Some of it is frustration — the specific kind of frustration you only feel about a place that produced you. Some of it is guilt, for having left, for having done well abroad, for eating at the restaurant with the generator and then getting on a plane. Some of it is the thing I don't have a word for — the dual citizenship of the mind, where half your reflexes are American and half are not, and you can't fully be either in either place.

What I've decided is that I don't need to untangle any of it to do the work. I just need to do the work.

Small, boring, repeatable. The bridges compound.
